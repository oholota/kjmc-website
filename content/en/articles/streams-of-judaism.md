Judaism is not one single thing. Over the centuries it has grown into several distinct streams, each with its own way of reading Scripture, keeping tradition, and relating to the modern world. Here is a short, friendly map — and where Messianic Judaism stands within it.

## Orthodox Judaism

The most traditional stream, holding the halacha (rabbinic law) as binding. It is itself very diverse — from intellectually-minded communities that prize study and reason, to the warm, joyful, song-filled world of **Hasidism**, to **Modern Orthodox** Jews who combine full observance with higher education and life in wider society. What unites them is the authority of the Talmud and halacha.

## Conservative Judaism (Masorti)

A "middle path" that emerged in the 19th century between Orthodoxy and Reform. It recognizes the authority of halacha but allows it to develop over time, adapting to new circumstances while keeping a strong link to tradition.

## Reform Judaism

Born in 19th-century Germany as a response to emancipation, when Jews gained civil rights and asked how to live as Jews in the modern world. Reform Judaism adapts tradition to contemporary life and emphasizes ethics, openness and social responsibility.

## Secular and cultural Jewish identity

Many Jewish people today connect to their heritage culturally rather than religiously — through family, language, history and the festivals — without a defined theology. Some of the most honest spiritual searches happen here, free of inherited filters.

## What they share — and the dividing line

For all their differences, these streams share a deep respect for Jewish life and, to varying degrees, the authority of rabbinic tradition. Historically they also share one thing in common: they have not embraced **Yeshua (Jesus) as the Messiah of Israel**. That is the line that sets Messianic Judaism apart.

## Where Messianic Judaism fits

Messianic Judaism is not a "denomination" alongside these — it is the continuation of the biblical faith that came before them all. The first followers of Yeshua were Jews who kept living as Jews while believing in Him as the promised Messiah. We stand in that first-century line: rooted in the Hebrew Scriptures, alive in the New Covenant, and centered on Yeshua.
