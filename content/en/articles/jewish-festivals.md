We celebrate God's festivals — not national or denominational holidays, but the times He Himself set apart and calls "My appointed times" (Leviticus 23:2). The point of every festival is not the ritual but God: the candles, the shofar, the sukkah, the matzah are simply tools that help us meet Him.

Each biblical festival has three dimensions: it **remembers** what God has done, it points to what He is **doing now**, and it looks forward to what He **will yet do**. The spring festivals point to the Messiah's first coming; the autumn festivals, to His return.

## Shabbat

The most important day of the week — set apart at creation (Genesis 2:1–3). Not merely a day of rest, but a weekly meeting with God. *(See our full article: [What Is Shabbat?](/en/articles/shabbat/))*

## Pesach (Passover)

The festival of deliverance from Egypt and the remembrance of the Passover lamb. For us it carries a double meaning: the historical exodus from slavery, and the prophetic sacrifice of Yeshua as the Lamb of God. He was crucified at the time the Passover lambs were slain, and rose on the day of the firstfruits.

## Counting the Omer & Shavuot

The 49 days from Pesach to Shavuot are a season of preparation that joins the exodus from Egypt to the giving of the Torah at Sinai. Shavuot celebrates both the giving of the Torah and the first harvest — and it was on this day that the Holy Spirit came upon Yeshua's disciples (Acts 2). The festival highlights the unity of Torah and Spirit.

## Rosh haShana

The day of blowing the shofar — the start of ten days of awe before Yom Kippur. A time of self-examination, repentance and preparing the heart for renewal.

## Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement)

The holiest day of the year: a day of fasting, prayer and deep repentance. For us it also points to the eternal sacrifice of Yeshua, our High Priest, who entered the Holy of Holies with His own blood (Hebrews 9:11–12).

## Sukkot (Feast of Tabernacles)

A festival of joy and thanksgiving, recalling Israel's dwelling in temporary booths under God's protection — a reminder that our true covering is His presence, not walls and roofs. A time of community, hospitality and gladness in the Lord.

## Hanukkah

The festival of rededication and light, recalling the cleansing of the Temple. For us it is a reminder that God's light cannot be put out — and that the rededicated Temple set the stage for the coming of the Messiah.

## Purim

The festival of God's deliverance of His people through Esther — a reminder that even when God seems silent, He is at work behind the scenes, faithful to His promises.

---

Above all, the festivals are not just memories — they are doorways into the living reality of God's plan, all of it pointing to the Messiah.
